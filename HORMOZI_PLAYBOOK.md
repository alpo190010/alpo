# The Alex Hormozi $100M playbook: every framework, rule, and formula

Alex Hormozi's three-book series forms a complete business operating system: **$100M Offers** teaches you to create offers people can't refuse, **$100M Leads** shows you how to get those offers in front of enough people, and **$100M Money Models** reveals how to sequence and structure offers so one customer funds the acquisition of two more. Together, they cover the full revenue engine — from first contact to lifetime value. What follows is a dense, actionable extraction of every major principle, framework, formula, and mistake-to-avoid from all three books.

# Book 1: $100M Offers (2021)
## "How to make offers so good people feel stupid saying no"

The core thesis is simple: **your offer — not your product, not your ads, not your sales skills — is the single greatest lever in your business.** A "Grand Slam Offer" puts you in a category of one where the prospect's only decision is between your offer and nothing. One great offer can fund a lifetime of wealth.

### The Value Equation: the crown jewel of the book

Every offer's perceived value is governed by four variables arranged in a single formula:

**Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)**

The numerator is what you increase; the denominator is what you decrease. Beginners obsess over the top (making bigger promises). **The best companies — Amazon, Apple — obsess over the bottom**, making results faster and nearly effortless. If you could reduce time delay and effort to zero, you'd have infinitely valuable product. Perception matters as much as reality: London's Underground boosted rider satisfaction more by adding dot-matrix arrival-time displays than by actually speeding up trains.

Each driver works as follows. *Dream Outcome* is the emotional, status-driven transformation your buyer envisions — frame it in terms of how others will perceive them, not just raw metrics ("your golf buddies' jaws will drop" beats "40 more yards of drive"). *Perceived Likelihood of Achievement* is certainty — boost it with proof, testimonials, case studies, and guarantees. *Time Delay* is the gap between purchase and result — always engineer an immediate quick win so clients feel momentum in the first days. *Effort & Sacrifice* is why done-for-you services outsell DIY, why supplements ($123B industry) outsell gym memberships ($62B), and why Xanax outsells meditation apps.

### Finding your starving crowd before building any offer

Market selection outranks everything. Hormozi's hierarchy: **Starving Crowd > Offer Strength > Persuasion Skills.** A mediocre offer in a desperate market beats a brilliant offer in a dead one. The four indicators of a great market are **massive pain** (the degree of pain is proportional to the price you can charge), **purchasing power** ("solve rich people's problems — they pay better"), **easy to target** (associations, mailing lists, social media groups), and **growing** (tailwinds beat headwinds).

Niching down is non-negotiable below $10M in revenue. A generic weight-loss program sells for $19. The same program repositioned for night-shift nurses sells for $1,997. Hormozi's niche formula: "I solve [this problem] for [this specific person] in [this unique way] that reverses [their deepest fear]."

### The five-step offer creation process

**Step 1: Identify the dream outcome.** Not your deliverable — the destination. Nobody wants a gym membership; they want to look great naked. Don't sell the plane flight; sell the vacation.

**Step 2: List every obstacle.** Write down everything that could prevent the customer from achieving their outcome, in the exact sequence they'd experience each problem. Think in insane detail. Every unsolved obstacle becomes a reason someone doesn't buy.

**Step 3: Reframe obstacles as solutions.** Add "how to" and reverse each problem. Then give each solution a compelling name. "Buying healthy food on a budget" becomes the "Foolproof Bargain Grocery System."

**Step 4: Design delivery vehicles using the Delivery Cube.** Brainstorm across six dimensions: group ratio (1-on-1, small group, one-to-many), effort level (DIY, done-with-you, done-for-you), support type (SMS, chat, phone, Zoom), format (live, recorded, written, video), speed/convenience (24/7, same-day, within an hour), and the 10x/1/10th test — ask what you'd deliver if your price were 10× higher, and what you'd deliver if it were 1/10th and had to be even more valuable.

**Step 5: Trim and stack.** Rate every delivery vehicle on value-to-customer versus cost-to-you. Kill high-cost/low-value items first, then low-cost/low-value. Keep everything that is **low-cost/high-value** (especially one-to-many solutions that scale infinitely) and selectively keep high-cost/high-value items. Stack what remains into a single, named offer that cannot be compared to anything else in the market.

### Premium pricing and the virtuous cycle

Hormozi's pricing philosophy: "Charge as high a price as you can say out loud without cracking a smile." **Never compete on price** — the competitors you'd be copying are broke. Dan Kennedy's rule applies: there's no strategic benefit to being the second-cheapest, but there is for being the most expensive.

Pricing triggers a cycle. The **vicious cycle**: cut prices → thinner margins → underinvest in delivery → worse results → bad reviews → salespeople lose conviction → forced to cut further. The **virtuous cycle**: charge more → invest in better fulfillment → clients get better results → stronger testimonials → more conviction → charge even more → attract the best clients. "Those who pay the most, pay the most attention" — higher prices actually increase the probability that clients succeed.

### The four offer enhancers

**Scarcity (limit quantity).** Fear of loss beats desire for gain. Tactics include total business caps ("only accepting X clients at this level"), growth-rate caps ("only 5 new clients per week — 3 spots already taken"), cohort caps, and the nuclear option: "once you leave, you can never come back." The most ethical scarcity strategy is honesty — simply tell people how close you are to your cap.

**Urgency (limit time).** Up to 50–60% of sales in a week-long campaign happen in the final four hours. Use rolling cohorts (miss Monday, wait until next Monday), seasonal windows, pricing urgency ("never raise prices without announcing it first — it shows strength and converts fence-sitters"), and exploding opportunities where the deal worsens each day they wait.

**Bonuses (stack value).** A single offer broken into component parts and stacked as individual bonuses is perceived as more valuable than the same offer presented as one thing. The value of bonuses should eclipse the value of the core offer. Eleven rules govern bonuses: name each one with the benefit in the title, prove its standalone dollar value, favor tools and checklists over additional training (lower effort = higher value per the equation), make each bonus address a specific obstacle, and **never discount the main offer — add a bonus instead** to close deals from a position of strength.

**Guarantees (reverse risk).** Risk reversal is the number-one conversion lever. The guarantee statement format: "If you don't get [X result] in [Y time], we will [Z]." The four types are *unconditional* (money back, no questions), *conditional* (Hormozi's favorite — tie the guarantee to specific actions the client must take, then make it "better than money back" such as a service guarantee where you work free until the result is achieved), *anti-guarantee* ("all sales final — if you need a guarantee, you're not our customer"), and *implied/performance-based* (revenue share, pay-per-result, ratchets). Stack multiple guarantees together for maximum effect.

### Naming offers with the MAGIC formula

Use three to five components: **M**ake a magnetic reason why (seasonal, event-based), **A**nnounce your avatar (who it's for), **G**ive them a goal (the dream outcome), **I**ndicate a time interval, **C**omplete with a container word (Blueprint, Challenge, Accelerator, System). Example: "The 6-Week Mommy Makeover Challenge." When offers fatigue, refresh in order: change creatives first, then body copy, then the headline/wrapper, then duration, then the enhancer, and finally the monetization structure.

### $100M Offers — DO this

- Pick a market with massive pain, purchasing power, easy targeting, and growth before designing any offer
- Niche down ruthlessly — become "the person" for a specific audience with a specific problem
- Engineer an immediate quick win so clients feel results in the first week
- Use the Delivery Cube's 10×/1/10th test to brainstorm wildly different delivery vehicles
- Stack bonuses until their combined value dwarfs the core offer's price
- Create conditional guarantees tied to client actions — these protect you while maximizing conversion
- Name every offer, bonus, and sub-component using the MAGIC formula
- Raise prices and invest the margin into better fulfillment — trigger the virtuous cycle
- Overdeliver massively on your first Grand Slam Offer to generate cash flow and testimonials

### $100M Offers — DON'T do this

- Don't compete on price — it's a race to the bottom and your "competitors" are broke
- Don't present your offer the same way competitors do — if the prospect can compare, you're commoditized
- Don't focus only on bigger claims (top of the Value Equation); reduce time delay and effort instead
- Don't use fake scarcity or countdown timers — credibility, once lost, is nearly impossible to rebuild
- Don't discount your core offer to close a deal — add a bonus instead
- Don't use unconditional money-back guarantees on high-ticket, high-fulfillment-cost services
- Don't niche-hop — commit and iterate until the Grand Slam Offer clicks
- Don't ignore obstacles in the customer journey — every unsolved problem is a reason someone doesn't buy

# Book 2: $100M Leads (2023)
## "How to get strangers to want to buy your stuff"

The premise: **advertising is just "making known."** The number-one reason businesses don't get enough leads is they don't advertise enough, full stop. This book provides the complete system for generating leads across every channel, scaling that generation, and eventually getting other people to do it for you.

### Foundations: leads, engaged leads, and the financial formula that governs everything

A *lead* is anyone you can contact. An **engaged lead** — someone who voluntarily gives you their information because they're interested in what you sell — is the true output of advertising. Most businesses confuse raw contacts with engaged leads and wonder why nothing converts.

The single most important metric in the book is the **LTGP:CAC ratio** (Lifetime Gross Profit divided by Customer Acquisition Cost). The target is **at least 3:1**. Below that, your business model is broken — not your ads. Hormozi's lifetime average return on advertising is **36:1**. The corollary principle is *client-financed acquisition*: structure your offers so the customer pays back the CAC within 30 days, freeing that capital to acquire the next customer immediately.

### Lead magnets: a complete solution to a narrow problem

A lead magnet is not a flimsy PDF. It should be **so valuable it could be charged for.** The analogy: salty pretzels at a bar solve hunger (narrow problem) but create thirst (the need for your core offer). A person who pays with their time now is more likely to pay with money later.

The seven steps to creating one: (1) Pick a narrow, meaningful problem whose solution naturally reveals a bigger problem your core offer solves. (2) Choose how to solve it — diagnose a problem they didn't know they had, offer a sample/trial of the core product, or give one step of a multi-step process. (3) Choose a delivery format — software/tools, information, services, or physical products. (4) A/B test the name relentlessly — David Ogilvy noted that **five times more people read the headline than the body**, so the title is 80% of the magnet's effectiveness. (5) Make it frictionless to consume — multiple formats, no downloads or account creation required. (6) Make it genuinely excellent — "give away the secrets, sell the implementation." Giving too little value is a far greater risk than giving too much. (7) End with a clear CTA that tells them exactly what to do next and gives a reason to do it now.

### The Core Four: the only four ways to advertise

Hormozi derives the Core Four from two axes — audience type (warm vs. cold) and communication style (one-to-one vs. one-to-many):

| | One-to-One | One-to-Many |
|---|---|---|
| **Warm Audience** | Warm Outreach | Post Free Content |
| **Cold Audience** | Cold Outreach | Run Paid Ads |

**Warm Outreach** is the starting point for every business. Compile every contact you have (aim for 1,000+), personalize each message, and reach out to **100 people per day**. Use the ACA framework in conversation: Acknowledge what they said, Compliment them, Ask a guiding question. After 3–4 exchanges, ask: "Do you know anyone who [specific struggle] looking to [dream outcome] in [time frame]?" Offer the first five clients free in exchange for using the product, giving feedback, and leaving a review. Then gradually increase price every five clients (80% off → 60% → 40% → full price). Benchmarks: ~20% reply rate, ~20% acceptance of a free offer, ~1 in 4 free acceptances converts to paid — yielding roughly 1 customer per 100 outreaches.

**Posting Free Content** builds the most valuable long-term asset: an audience. Content is structured as **Hook → Retain → Reward.** The hook grabs attention (measured by % who start consuming). Retention sustains engagement through lists, steps, or stories. The reward delivers on the hook's promise — measured by shares and return visits. The key metric isn't views; it's **audience growth**. Hormozi gained 200K followers in 12 months, then 1.2M in the next six months by increasing output 10×. When his paid ads were shut off, 78% of clients had consumed at least two long-form pieces before booking a call. Monetize with a give:ask ratio of at least 10:1 — provide so much free value the audience initiates the sale.

**Cold Outreach** taps an unlimited pool of strangers. Solve three problems: build targeted lists (scrapers, brokers, manual research), overcome distrust by delivering "Big Fast Value" upfront, and maximize volume through automation. Write at a **third-grade reading level** — Hormozi found this boosted response rates 50%. Expect 1–5% of cold contacts to become engaged leads. Phone benchmarks: 20% pick-up × 25% accept lead magnet = 4 engaged leads per hour. DMs are the highest-converting channel if you use personalized voice or video messages.

**Paid Ads** trade money for guaranteed reach. Hormozi's ad framework has three elements: Call Out (labels, yes-questions, if-then statements to grab attention), Value (what-who-when benefits), and CTA. Paid ads go through three phases: *Track Money* (set up measurement), *Lose Money* (test 10 ads at $100 each — expect 9 to fail, but the 1 winner funds everything), and *Print Money* (scale winners aggressively). An eight-figure entrepreneur gave Hormozi "permission to fail" by dedicating a percentage of budget to relentless testing — Hormozi tripled his ad spend and revenue jumped from **$400K/month to $4M/month**.

### The "More → Better → New" scaling framework

When lead generation plateaus, apply this sequence in strict order. **More** first: do more of what's already working. Follow the **Rule of 100** — 100 primary actions per day per channel (100 outreaches, 100 minutes of content creation, or $100 in ad spend). Hormozi distributed 300 flyers and got zero leads; a mentor told him to distribute 5,000 per day and results exploded. **Better** second: identify the single step where the most leads drop off and optimize only that constraint, testing one variable per week. **New** last: only after more and better are exhausted, add new placements within the current platform, then new platforms, then entirely new Core Four methods. Every "new" channel comes with a learning curve that fragments attention.

### Lead Getters: four ways to get others advertising for you

The highest leverage isn't doing the Core Four yourself — it's recruiting **Lead Getters** who do it on your behalf.

**Customer Referrals** are the most underused growth engine. When Hormozi's ads were shut off for two weeks, the business still generated $500K+/week from referrals alone. The formula: Goodwill = Value Delivered − Price Charged. The wider that gap, the more referrals flow. The two reasons you don't get referrals: your product isn't good enough, or you don't ask. Incentivize both the referrer and the referred, and frame the request with a specific ask: "Who is one person you know who [specific struggle]?"

**Employees** turn a high-paying job into a valuable, saleable asset. Recruit using the same Core Four methods. Train with the **3Ds: Document, Demonstrate, Duplicate.** Measure employee-driven leads against the same 3:1 LTGP:CAC threshold.

**Agencies** should be treated as high-priced, short-term accelerated learning tools — never permanent dependencies. Tell them upfront you want to learn their methods, have your internal team document everything, set a six-month deadline, then replace them once the skill is internalized.

**Affiliates** are independent businesses marketing your offer to their audience. Hormozi's Prestige Labs generated millions monthly through affiliates without running his own ads. Qualify affiliates by making them customers first or requiring paid certification. Launch with the **Whisper-Tease-Shout** sequence: hint that something is coming, build anticipation with previews, then go full launch. For long-term flow, integrate your product into the affiliate's own core offer so your success becomes necessary for theirs.

### The seven-level growth roadmap

The implementation order matters: (1) warm outreach for first clients, (2) post free content to build an audience, (3) hire employees to do what you're doing, (4) improve the product until referrals flow consistently, (5) expand to multiple Core Four methods and platforms, (6) hire specialized executives, (7) the full $100M machine with all Core Four methods and all Lead Getters operating simultaneously.

### $100M Leads — DO this

- Start with warm outreach immediately — it's free, reliable, and builds skills for every other channel
- Follow the Rule of 100: 100 primary actions per day per channel, every single day
- Create lead magnets that are genuinely worth paying for — mediocre free content actively harms your brand
- Track LTGP:CAC ratio as your north-star metric; don't scale until it's at least 3:1
- Write cold outreach at a third-grade reading level for maximum response rates
- Give yourself "permission to fail" with paid ads — test relentlessly and expect most ads to lose
- Ask for referrals systematically with specific language and dual-sided incentives
- Use agencies to learn, not as permanent crutches — set a six-month exit deadline
- Master one platform before expanding ("depth then width")

### $100M Leads — DON'T do this

- Don't start with paid ads before mastering organic and outreach methods
- Don't confuse a bad business model (low LTGP) with bad ads (high CAC) — fix the model first
- Don't send generic copy-paste messages in cold or warm outreach
- Don't chase "new" channels before exhausting "more" and "better" on existing ones
- Don't fear giving away too much value in lead magnets — giving too little is the real risk
- Don't rely on a single lead generation channel — diversification prevents catastrophic failure
- Don't stop too soon — wrong action in wrong volume still fails; volume is the variable most people underestimate
- Don't create content sporadically — consistency and volume compound; audience growth is the metric, not virality
- Don't become permanently dependent on any agency for a core business function

# Book 3: $100M Money Models (2025)
## "How to make your business print money"

Released in August 2025 to a Guinness World Record **2.9 million copies sold on day one**, this book completes the trilogy. Where *Offers* teaches what to sell and *Leads* teaches how to find buyers, *Money Models* teaches **the sequence and structure of offers that turns one customer's payment into the acquisition funding for two more.** The root cause of 82% of business failures is poor cash flow management — and the fix is a deliberate money model.

### The core principle: the 30-day profit rule

A Money Model is a deliberate sequence of offers — what you offer, when, and how — designed to extract maximum revenue per customer as fast as possible. The non-negotiable rule: **profit from one customer within 30 days must exceed the cost to acquire and serve that customer.** When this holds, cash stops being the growth constraint. The business scales as fast as operations allow.

The compounding math is powerful. If you make customers **2× as valuable**, acquire **2× as many**, and get them to pay at **2× the speed**, the business grows **8× as fast** (2 × 2 × 2). Triple all three and growth multiplies **27×**.

### The three stages: Get Cash → Get More Cash → Get the Most Cash

**Stage I — Get Cash (Attraction Offers).** Turn strangers into first-time paying customers. Five specific frameworks:

The **"Win Your Money Back" challenge** has the customer pay upfront (e.g., $500); if they hit their goal, they receive the money back as store credit. Most won't claim it; those who do become your best testimonials. One gym went from $300 CAC with 3-month breakeven to **$200+ cash positive on day one** and expanded from 1 to 6 locations.

**Giveaways** advertise a grand prize, collect leads, and offer a discounted core product to every non-winner. Skool used this to grow from 1M to 15M+ users in 18 months. **Decoy Offers** place a cheap, stripped-down version next to the premium option, making the premium feel obviously better. **Buy X Get Y Free** reframes price without discounting the headline number — the "free" item should be perceived as more valuable than the paid one. **Pay Less Now or Pay More Later** adds a 5–20% premium for delayed action, combining time scarcity with rational savings logic.

**Stage II — Get More Cash (Upsell and Downsell Offers).** Maximize 30-day profit from each customer.

The four upsell frameworks are the *Classic Upsell* ("want fries with that?" — one complementary add-on, one click), the *Menu Upsell* (unsell what they don't need, prescribe what they do, offer A or B instead of yes/no, make payment easy — this took Prestige Labs from **$270K/month to $1.6M/month in 8 weeks** with the same product), the *Anchor Upsell* (present a premium tier at 2–4× the core price so the mid-tier feels like a deal), and the *Rollover Upsell* (credit a previous purchase toward the next, like applying a $750 HVAC maintenance fee toward a new system).

A critical insight from the book: **"The only thing worse than having a $100 customer say no to a $1,000 offer is having a $1,000 customer say yes to a $100 offer. You lose $100 on the first deal, but $900 on the second."** Always present the highest-value option first.

Three downsell frameworks recover revenue from "no" or "not yet" responses. The *Payment Plan Downsell* keeps the same product with easier terms (charge a 5–20% premium for plans to cover risk). The *Feature Downsell* offers a lower-priced package with reduced scope but the same core outcome — include an ascension coupon to upgrade within 14 days. The *Free Trial Downsell* puts a card on file and ensures the trial includes the product's "aha moment" with guided onboarding.

**Stage III — Get the Most Cash (Continuity Offers).** Maximize lifetime value through recurring revenue. The *Continuity Bonus Offer* gives an incredibly valuable bonus for free — but only if they join the recurring program. The bonus should be worth more than the first several payments. Gym Launch used this to go from one-time $16K sales to **$1.76M/month in recurring revenue within six months**, eventually selling for $46.2M. *Continuity Discounts* lock in preferred pricing for subscribers with credits that expire monthly. *Waived Fee Offers* waive setup costs in exchange for a recurring commitment.

### Unit economics: the formulas that drive every decision

The essential formulas from the book:

- **Unit Profit = Price − COGS**
- **CAC = Total Acquisition Spend ÷ New Customers Acquired**
- **Payback Period = CAC ÷ Monthly Gross Profit Per Customer** (target: ≤ 30 days)
- **LTV = Average Revenue Per Customer Per Month × Average Customer Lifespan in Months**
- **LTGP = LTV minus all fulfillment/delivery costs** (Hormozi insists on using gross profit, not revenue)

The **CAC Dashboard Waterfall** tracks cost at each stage: Cost Per Click → Cost Per Lead → Cost Per Show → Cost Per Opportunity → Cost Per Acquisition. This reveals exactly where money leaks.

LTV:CAC ratio benchmarks vary by business type: **3:1** for fully automated businesses (SaaS), **6:1** when one human touchpoint exists, **9:1+** for manual sales and service delivery, and **20:1–30:1** for manual businesses targeting cold markets. Hormozi's personal best was **100:1** in the first year of Gym Launch ($100K spent, $10M generated).

Gross profit margin targets: **≥80%** for service businesses, **≥40%** for physical products. If unit economics work for one customer, more customers make more money. If they don't, stop scaling immediately.

### The money model design rules

Hormozi prescribes strict implementation principles. **One promise per offer, one action per step, one metric per stage.** Simple beats clever — one avatar, one painful promise, one page, one CTA, one follow-up plan. Speed to live matters — imperfect and launched today beats perfect and never shipped. Numbers drive decisions: if CAC is less than gross profit contribution within the payback period target, scale; if not, fix. **Master one offer type completely before introducing additional offers.** And always follow the sequence: get flow → monetize flow → add friction.

The book's meta-demonstration was the launch itself. The free mega-event served as the attraction offer. "Pay Less Now" VIP pre-orders created urgency. A "Donate 200 Books" offer at $5,998 (where buyers use the books as their own lead-gen tools) served as an upsell. An $18K advisory package topped the stack. Result: **$6.5M in pre-order revenue before the event and $81M claimed on launch day.**

### $100M Money Models — DO this

- Structure every offer sequence around the 30-day profit rule: one customer's profit must fund acquiring at least two more
- Build the full offer stack — Attraction → Upsell → Downsell → Continuity — but perfect one stage at a time
- Always present the highest-value option first; downsell only after a "no"
- Use the Menu Upsell framework: unsell what they don't need, prescribe what they do, offer A or B (not yes/no)
- Add a continuity bonus worth more than the first several recurring payments to seed subscriptions
- Track the full CAC Dashboard Waterfall (CPC → CPL → CPS → CPOpp → CPA) to find exactly where money leaks
- Calculate LTV using gross profit, not revenue — revenue-based LTV creates a dangerously false picture
- Announce next month's bonus at renewal time to pre-empt churn before it happens
- Charge a 5–20% premium on payment plans to offset the risk and admin cost of split payments

### $100M Money Models — DON'T do this

- Don't scale a business with broken unit economics — if one unit doesn't profit, more units just accelerate losses
- Don't chase the cheapest leads — optimize for the best LTV:CAC ratio instead
- Don't confuse a broken money model with a broken product — the same product with a better sequence of offers can generate 5× the revenue
- Don't implement the full money model at once — perfect one offer, measure quarterly, then layer the next
- Don't create multiple different products when you can present the same product in multiple ways (upsell, downsell, continuity)
- Don't confuse revenue with gross profit when calculating LTV — this single error kills more scaling attempts than any other
- Don't use hard-sell tactics; make offers available when customers have a problem and let the value speak
- Don't let a past bad investment prevent a future good one — evaluate each decision independently

## The unified operating system

Read together, the three books form a single loop: create an offer so good it sells itself (*Offers*), put that offer in front of enough people using all four advertising methods and four types of lead getters (*Leads*), and structure the offer sequence so every customer funds the next two within 30 days (*Money Models*). The entrepreneurs who execute all three — irresistible offers, relentless lead generation at volume, and airtight unit economics — are the ones who build businesses worth $100M and beyond. The ones who stall almost always have a gap in one of the three. Identify which book addresses your bottleneck, fix that constraint first, and then move to the next.
